Main Progression Rules

Stay in the target rep range

Do not rush weight increases before the reps and form are stable. The rep range tells you where you should live before earning a heavier load.

Keep 1–2 reps in reserve

Most sets should feel challenging but not like an all-out max. You should usually finish knowing you had a little more left.

Increase load only when earned

Once all sets reach the top of the rep range with good form, move the weight up next time. That is your main progression trigger.

If form breaks, stay there

If reps look sloppy, range gets shorter, or you need momentum, keep the same weight next session and clean up technique first.

How to Progress a Lift

Example: Hip Thrust 4 × 8–12

Stay with the same weight until all 4 working sets reach 12 reps with good control and full lockout.

Week 1: 10, 10, 9, 8
Week 2: 11, 10, 10, 9
Week 3: 12, 11, 10, 10
Week 4: 12, 12, 12, 12 → increase the weight next session

When Not to Increase Weight

You shortened the range of motion to finish the set.
You had to swing, jerk, or rush the reps.
The target muscle stopped doing the work and everything moved into your joints or lower back.
You could not match the minimum reps for the planned sets.
You are still sore and under-recovered every week from the same exercise.

What to Track Weekly

Training Log

Track exercise, weight used, reps completed, and quick notes on how the set felt.

Body Check-In

Take photos every 2–4 weeks under similar lighting and compare shape changes, not daily fluctuations.

Recovery

Note sleep quality, soreness, and energy. Progress gets slower when recovery is poor.

Consistency

Your body changes more from months of good work than one perfect week. Aim for repeatable progress.

Simple Weekly Checklist

Did I complete all 4 training days?
Did I hit my protein target most days?
Did I improve reps, load, or quality on at least a few key lifts?
Did I keep cardio supportive instead of exhausting?
Do I feel recovered enough to repeat the plan next week?
